Should I cancel my Israeli tax residency and/or get an Argentine DNI?

A plain-English breakdown of the rules, the risks, and the pros & cons — for someone who is an Israeli citizen spending ~80–90% of the year outside Israel, mostly in Argentina.

⚠️ The single most important fact: Israel and Argentina have no double-taxation treaty. Normally a treaty has a "tie-breaker" that decides which country gets to tax you. With no treaty, if both countries decide you are their resident, you can be taxed twice on the same income, with limited relief. Any plan must make sure you are clearly a resident of one country, not stuck between the two.

🇮🇱 How Israel decides if you still owe tax

The test
Your "center of life" — where your family, money, social ties and home really are. Day-counting is a starting point, not the whole story.
Day presumptions
Presumed resident if 183+ days in Israel in a year, or 30+ days this year and 425+ days over 3 years. (A 2025 draft law would make these harder to argue against.)
If resident
Taxed on worldwide income.
If non-resident
Taxed only on Israeli-source income.
Leaving is not automatic
Even at 80–90% abroad, Israel does not "release" you on its own. You must prove your center of life has truly moved out — and trigger the exit tax below.
Exit tax 🔑
On the day you stop being a resident, Israel treats all your worldwide assets as sold and taxes the gains (roughly 25%–50%). You can usually defer payment until you actually sell, but the bill is locked in.
Coming back later
If you ever return after 10+ years abroad, you can get generous "returning resident" tax breaks — worth protecting.

🇦🇷 How Argentina decides if you owe tax

DNI ≠ tax 🔑
A DNI is migration paperwork, not a tax status. You can hold a DNI and not be a tax resident — these are two separate systems.
When you become a tax resident
When you get permanent residence, OR after 12 months of legal residence (short trips out, ≤90 days, don't reset the clock).
If tax resident
Taxed on worldwide income (progressive, up to 35%) plus a wealth tax (Bienes Personales) on your worldwide assets — though there's a tax-free minimum and rates are dropping toward 0.25% by 2027.
If not tax resident
Taxed only on Argentine-source income.
Practical reality
High inflation and historic currency controls make Argentine tax residency operationally messy compared to Israel.

Decision 1 — Cancel Israeli tax residency Israel

✓ Pros
  • Future worldwide income (incl. business / crypto / investments) no longer taxed by Israel.
  • Matches reality — you're already mostly abroad.
  • Stops the "am I still a resident?" uncertainty piling up each year.
✗ Cons
  • Exit tax now: deemed sale of all assets, gains taxed 25–50%.
  • Must genuinely cut ties (home, bank, family base) — half-measures fail and you stay taxable.
  • You need a new clear tax home, or you're a resident of nowhere — auditable and risky with no treaty.
  • Lose easy access to local benefits; returning-resident perks only after 10 yrs out.

Decision 2 — Get an Argentine DNI / become tax resident there Argentina

✓ Pros
  • Legal stability where you actually live — no more visa/tourist juggling.
  • A DNI alone does not trigger tax residency, so you can get the document without immediate tax cost.
  • Gives you a clear "other country" to point to when leaving Israel.
✗ Cons
  • If you cross into Argentine tax residency: worldwide income tax up to 35%.
  • Wealth tax on your worldwide assets — Israel has no equivalent annual wealth tax.
  • Inflation + currency controls = real-world friction.
  • No treaty: a bad overlap year could mean double tax.

🧭 Three realistic paths

Path A — Stay an Israeli resident, do nothing

Keep paying Israeli tax on worldwide income. No exit tax, no Argentine wealth tax. Simple, but you keep paying Israel even though you barely live there.

Best if your assets/gains are large (exit tax would hurt) or you plan to move back.

Path B — Become a clean Argentine tax resident, cut Israel

One clear home: Argentina. Pay exit tax to Israel once, then live under Argentine rules. Watch the wealth tax and inflation.

Best if you're committed to Argentina long-term and your asset gains are modest (low exit tax).

Path C — Get the DNI, but don't trigger Argentine tax residency, while exiting Israel

Tempting "low-tax" route, but with no treaty it can leave you a resident of nowhere — the riskiest spot if either country audits you.

Only viable with a professional structuring it carefully. Don't freelance this one.

✅ Sensible next steps

  1. Estimate your exit tax first. List your assets and unrealized gains — this single number decides whether leaving Israel is cheap or expensive right now.
  2. Decide your "one home". Pick Israel or Argentina as your clear tax residence. Avoid being in-between.
  3. Separate the two questions. Getting a DNI (migration) can be done now and is low-risk; changing tax residency is the heavy, irreversible move.
  4. Get one cross-border advisor who knows both Israeli and Argentine rules — because there's no treaty, you can't rely on a tie-breaker to fix mistakes.
